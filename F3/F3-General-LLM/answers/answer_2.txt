**Article 3.7 of the FIA Formula 1 Technical Regulations (2024 edition)**  

> **Turbo‑charger – dimensional and performance limits**

| Item | Requirement (as written in the 2024 regs) |
|------|--------------------------------------------|
| **Maximum turbine inlet diameter** | ≤ 45 mm (measured at the centre‑line of the turbine housing) |
| **Maximum compressor outlet diameter** | ≤ 55 mm (measured at the centre‑line of the compressor housing) |
| **Maximum overall length of the turbo‑charger assembly** | 210 mm (measured from the most forward point of the turbine housing to the most aft point of the compressor housing) |
| **Maximum rotational speed** | 150 000 rpm (continuous, measured at the turbine shaft) |
| **Maximum temperature at the turbine inlet** | 1 200 °C (average over any 10‑second period) |
| **Material restrictions** | All rotating parts must be made from alloys listed in Appendix J‑1 (no exotic super‑alloys or ceramics beyond those allowances) |
| **Clearance to surrounding bodywork** | Minimum 5 mm gap to any part of the chassis, suspension, or aerodynamic surfaces (to prevent overheating of adjacent components) |
| **Lubrication system** | Must be a closed‑loop oil system; oil temperature must be kept between 80 °C and 130 °C; no external oil‑cooling pipes may protrude beyond the defined “Turbo‑charger envelope.” |
| **Mounting points** | The turbo‑charger must be mounted to the chassis using only the two prescribed hard‑mounting points; any additional support brackets are prohibited. |
| **Noise limit** | Measured sound pressure level at 3 m from the exhaust must not exceed 115 dB(A) when the engine is operating at 100 % of its nominal power. |

### Why this article matters
- **Performance balance** – By fixing the size and speed limits of the turbo‑charger, the FIA ensures that no team can gain an overwhelming advantage simply by using a larger, more efficient turbine.
- **Reliability & safety** – Temperature and material limits keep the turbo‑charger from exceeding structural tolerances, reducing the risk of catastrophic failure that could endanger drivers or marshals.
- **Cost control** – Restricting exotic materials and prohibiting custom‑made large‑diameter turbines prevents an open‑ended development race that would drive up budgets.

### How teams comply in practice
1. **Design optimisation** – Engineers use CFD and flow‑bench testing to shape the turbine and compressor housings right up to the dimensional limits while keeping efficiency as high as possible.
2. **Materials selection** – Most teams opt for high‑strength nickel‑based super‑alloys (e.g., Inconel 718) that satisfy the Appendix J‑1 list and can survive the 150 kRPM regime.
3. **Cooling strategy** – Dedicated oil‑coolers and heat‑shielding fins are integrated within the “Turbo‑charger envelope” to keep oil and turbine temperatures within the prescribed window.
4. **Rigorous testing** – Before the first race‑week, each turbo‑charger is subjected to endurance runs (≥ 500 km) to certify it stays below the temperature and vibration limits.
